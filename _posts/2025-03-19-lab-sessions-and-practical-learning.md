There is a particular kind of learning that only happens when you are sitting in front of a computer, trying to make something work, and it is not working. That is the kind of learning that happens in lab sessions — and it is, without question, the most effective kind I have experienced at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus this semester.

The lab component of our programming course was where theory became real. In lectures, Dr. Bilal Ahmad would explain a concept — a loop structure, a function, a query — with clarity and precision. But it was only in the lab, when I had to implement that concept myself, from scratch, under time pressure, that it truly settled into my understanding. The gap between following an explanation and producing working code on your own is enormous, and bridging that gap is exactly what lab sessions are designed for.

![Coding in Lab](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712202-14dd9538aa97?w=800)

The lab environment also taught me how to handle failure productively. In a lecture, when something does not make sense, you can note it down and move on. In a lab, when your code does not compile or your query returns unexpected results, you have to stop and figure out why. That process of debugging — reading error messages, tracing logic, identifying where the reasoning broke down — is one of the most valuable skills a programmer can develop, and it cannot be taught from a textbook. It has to be experienced.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad's emphasis on practical application reflects his broader philosophy as a researcher. His work in AI and ML model training, particularly with medical datasets, is fundamentally empirical — it is about running experiments, observing results, and iterating based on what the data reveals. That same iterative, hands-on mindset is what he brings to his teaching, and the lab sessions are where that mindset is most directly transmitted to students.

![Student in Computer Lab](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1498050108023-c5249f4df085?w=800)

By the end of the semester, I had written programs that I genuinely could not have imagined writing in week one. That progress — visible, concrete, measurable — is one of the most motivating experiences of my time at university so far. Lab sessions made it possible, and I look forward to every practical session in the semesters ahead.
