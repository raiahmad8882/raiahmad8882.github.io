Before university, I thought programming was just about typing code into a computer and watching it run. That idea was shattered within the first week of Dr. Bilal Ahmad's Programming Fundamentals class at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus. Programming, as I quickly learned, is a discipline of thinking — and the code is simply how that thinking gets expressed.

We started with the absolute basics — variables, data types, input and output — and at first it felt almost too simple. But Dr. Bilal Ahmad was deliberate about this. He wanted us to understand every building block completely before moving on, because in programming, a shaky foundation causes everything built on top of it to collapse. That patience in the early weeks paid off enormously when the material became more complex.

![C++ Code Practice](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1515879218367-8466d910aaa4?w=800)

Loops were the first real challenge. Understanding how a for loop, a while loop, and a do-while loop differ — not just in syntax but in the situations each one is best suited for — required a kind of logical thinking I had not been asked to apply before. I remember spending an entire evening just writing different loop variations, testing their behavior, and trying to internalize the pattern. That evening of frustration was one of the most productive of my semester.

Functions came next, and with them came a concept that changed how I think about problem-solving entirely — breaking a big problem into smaller, independent pieces. Each function does one job and does it well. That principle, which Dr. Bilal Ahmad emphasized repeatedly, is not just a programming concept. It is a life skill. When I face a complex assignment or project now, I automatically think about how to decompose it into manageable components.

![Student Coding](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1542831371-29b0f74f9713?w=800)

Arrays were the final major concept that truly stretched my understanding this semester. Storing and accessing multiple values under a single name, iterating through them, sorting them — these operations form the backbone of almost every real program. Dr. Bilal Ahmad, whose research in AI and ML relies heavily on working with large datasets, often reminded us that arrays are our first introduction to thinking about data in bulk — a concept that becomes central in machine learning and data science.

By the end of the semester, I could write programs that solved real problems — not just textbook exercises. That ability, built step by step under Dr. Bilal Ahmad's guidance, is something I will carry into every course and project that follows. Programming Fundamentals was not just a subject. It was the beginning of a completely new way of thinking.
